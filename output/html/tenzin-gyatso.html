Tenzin Gyatso

Religion

The Dalai Lama is the spiritual leader of the Gelug School of Tibetan Buddhism.

Political Views

The Dalai Lama is the political leader of the Tibetan Government in Exile.

Dalai Lama Tenzin Gyatso was born Lhamo Dhondup in Taktser, Amdo in northeastern Tibet in 1935. His full title is His Holiness the 14th Dalai Lama, Tenzin Gyatso.1

The Dalai Lama is a Tibetan Buddhist. More specifically, he is part of a sect of Tibetan Buddhism called “Gelug,” sometimes referred to as Yellow Hat Tibetan Buddhism.

For a more complete overview of Tibetan Buddhism or Gelug Tibetan Buddhism, follow the links to their respective Wikipedia articles. Or explore the reference section. But essentially, Gelug Buddhism differentiates itself from other sects by its focus on the philosophical idea of “the Void,” or emptiness.2 Furthermore, they advocate a systematic, stage-based approach to attaining enlightenment.3

The Dalai Lama, as the spiritual leader of this sect of Buddhism, is understandably a devout follower of its teachings. However, he is no evangelist, does not seem to view his beliefs as greater or more true than others, and often advocates the followings and teachings of other religions as they are similar in spirit to his own. For example, he said:

All major religious traditions carry basically the same message, that is love, compassion and forgiveness the important thing is they should be part of our daily lives.4

This essentially summarizes the the crux of the Dalai Lama’s emphasis. His is a philosophy of compassion, understanding, tolerance, forgiveness, self-discipline and contentment.5

Of the Dalai Lama’s three main objectives, the second is “the promotion of religious harmony and understanding among the worldâ€™s major religious traditions.”6

Forced into geo-politics

The Dalai Lama’s office is inherently a political one. Not only is he the spiritual leader of Tibetan Buddhists, he is their spokesperson to the world. And Tenzin Gyatso, of the 14 Dalai Lamas to date, has been required to engage with governments and legislative bodies the most–by far.

In 1950, the Chinese government under Mao Zedong forcefully took over the sovereign nation of Tibet. Nine years later, the Dalai Lama fled to India along with more than 100,000 other Tibetans.7

In the decades since, The Dalai Lama has set up a Tibetan government in exile in northern India and traveled the world many times over in hopes of garnering enough international support to pressure China into giving the country back to its original inhabitants–to no avail.8 He has said:

The Chinese claimed that they came to Tibet to ‘liberate’ us from the past and modernize the country. In fact they have brought the greatest suffering to our nation in its 2100 years of history.9

The Dalai Lama also adopted a multinational democratic government for Tibetans in exile, the first in Tibetan history–ironically after it was no longer a country. For his efforts, the UN adopted three resolutions against China and the Dalai Lama was awarded (among other things) the Nobel Peace Prize in 1989. Most notably, the Dalai Lama outlined a vision for the future of Tibet that included the nation as a sort of Switzerland-in-Asia, or, as the Dalai Lama calls it, a “zone of peace” in central Asia.10 So far, China has refused to cooperate.

Interestingly, the Dalai Lama himself has questioned the future of his own office in both spiritual and political terms. Without Tibet, he fears, there is no point. He said:

The Dalai Lama office was an institution created to benefit others. It is possible that it will soon have outlived its usefulness.11

It would be a sad state of affairs for Tibetan Buddhists, advocates of peace and freedom, and sovereignty in general if this man and his office were to give up.
